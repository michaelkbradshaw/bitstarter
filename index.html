CyberKnight

Beat a Video Game, Earn College Credit in Computer Science.

Cyberknight is a “serious computer game” where students get the equivalent of an Introduction to Computer Science class without the presence of an instructor. We envision that high school students who complete the game will earn college credit equivalent to a first-semester college-level computer science class like the AP Computer Science A Test.

Students will learn to program in Python, an elegant but powerful scripting language as they overcome obstacles, gather allies and discover the truth behind the Emptor invasion. The final level will be used to test student ability and will be proctored using online tools.

The World of Aedifex.

Aedifex is a world not so different from our own. It is filled with dangerous beasts, wicked traps, and magic. But these things do not make that world so different from our own. Our world has all of these things for those that know how to look.

What makes Aedifex different is its people. They live and breath the Art of Creation. To clearly state a thing makes that thing happen. This ability is know as Cyber- “to control”. Children of Aedifex pick up the Art the way that children of our world learn how to walk and talk. In fact the People Aedifex, use the art to walk, talk, and interact in their world.

FAQ

Are you serious?

Yes.

Cyberknight is led by professor of computer science Dr. Michael K. Bradshaw. Over the past three years, Dr. Bradshaw has led an interdisciplinary group of volunteer students who have created the engine, storyline, and gameplay ideas.

Why a video game?

There are other alternatives, books, websites and online courses. But learning computer science is the very essence of problem solving and that is hard stuff. Even in top colleges, the drop out rate for computer science courses remains high.

With a critical shortage of computer scientists around the world and an even more critical shortage of teachers in computer science we need to reach out for new tools to reach every soul that wants to learn but lacks the resources for one-on-one instruction.

How much will the game cost?

The game will be free to download and play. The game engine itself is open sourced and available for researchers and educators to create their own games.

Students that wish to play the final level for college credit (and to see how the game ends) will pay a fee to access that content. Early supporters will be offered fee discounts and access to materials in the beta testing. Friend us on Facebook to reserve your space.
